You are a young adult. You are upset about things going on in America. Maybe you are angry, scared, confused, depressed -- or maybe you feel a strange joy about everything burning down (I've certainly felt this at points). It's possible you feel many conflicting things; it's understandable, too, if you're just worn out, and you feel nothing at all.

Maybe you have some ideas on how to fix our deep, structural problems; perhaps, if everyone just adopted the tenets of Marxist socialism, all of our problems would be solved. Maybe big government is the problem; if all the people with power and wealth just left us alone, we'd be able to fend for ourselves in peace. Maybe you're already doing good work at a business, philanthropy, or in the government, but you're troubled at how out-of-touch the rest of America seems. Maybe you've been radicalized into a weird internet philosophy, like neoreaction, or you listen to popular cathartic podcasts like Chapo Trap House (I love those guys).

Maybe you actually have no idea how to fix things. You keep your head down, try to avoid the news and politics, and just focus on your work, your friends, and your health (I think this is a healthy response!). Maybe you're just utterly depressed about the state of everything; you're a doomer, part of you just wants to see everything crumble, and the only activity you have energy for is laying down and rotting (trust me, I've been there too).

I'm here to tell you that I……...have absolutely no idea how to fix anything in America. Honestly, we're probably boned. The events of 2020 -- covid, BLM, whatever the hell is going on in Washington DC -- have just been mind-boggling, terrifying, depressing, and horrific beyond comprehension. Even worse, 2020 has arrived after decades of deterioration of American ideas and institutions; on the global stage, we are now a complete joke. Especially in the eyes of China; a new great power that challenges us in ways we've never had to contend with before.

I don't know how to fix America, but I do know one thing -- the boomers definitely won't pull us out of this one. I have no problem with boomers; they're just old people, and eventually, boomerdom will come for us all. They've spent all their lives fighting the battles of the 20th century, and their effort has kept us safe* and (relatively***) prosperous, while we watch Twitch streams, struggle to find a rewarding career, try to find love, and argue with each other about how best we should treat everybody.

And honestly? None of this was our fault. We were babies, kids, teengaers, and fresh college students, as the grownups -- mostly through benign negligence -- careened the American empire into the ground. But every year that passes, and the gerontocracy continues to prove its inability to deal with the 21st century (especially with the rise of digital technology), it increasingly becomes our responsibility to fix it.

Young people are way smarter, more empathetic, and more capable than the grownups imagine. But the boomers have one big advantage over us; they are, for the very large part, masters of practicing civility.

This webpage is a short series of essays on the concept of civics, specifically targeted to the young adults of America. Briefly put, civics is the art of getting along in a democracy. It's quite possible that democracy itself is over (we discuss in length in the last piece). But for now, if young people want to make effective change, to make our slice of the world a better place, we need to practice being good citizens. And here are some ideas on how we might do that.

==

This essay series is five parts. It starts with a meditation on black lives matter; the brutal death of George Floyd kicked off an incredible and dizzying series of events in June. It was this event that inspired me to begin writing; I wanted to do something, but couldn't clarify exactly what I could positively contribute. By the way; black lives matter. It's okay to say it.

The second essay is a whirlwind tour of all the critical -- and potentially lethal -- layers of crisis we face as a society. Many of these issues are purposefully downplayed in popular media, with the (admirable) intent of protecting people from mass panic. I actually agree with the principle of preventing mass panic, but at some point, we will have to learn about and face these problems head-on. If you're not used to ruminating on the collapse of civilization, this essay has the potential to cause anxiety.

The third essay is actually a bit of a detour (this was originally supposed to be three parts), on the recent developments in a niche internet community I've been a part of for years. If you want a window into the digital culture that raised me personally, this essay could be illustrative. It also contains some general considerations on the trials and tribulations of being a public, influential figure (in short, it's bad; if you can at all help it, don't become a public figure).

The fourth essay ends with a grand discussion of different types of political systems. American-style democracy, for all the good it has done, is still just an experiment. It might not actually be around forever. We consider the alternative of totalitarianism, which is the logical step backwards if democratic-style governance proves itself to be ultimately infeasible. The meat of my argument is here; what it means to be a good citizen, and how we might get there.

Finally, I make a brief note on something important to me.
